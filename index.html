// Should I sell to government or commercial first?
Four guests across the catalog have weighed in on this. The strongest signal: pick a side and focus.
Jackson Moses · Founder & MP, Silent Ventures EP. 94 · 35:15

“Dual use is a hedge word, it’s optionality — hey, this company can be a commercial outperformer or a government outperformer or both, and we’re going to look brilliant. Companies get slaughtered.”

His framing for founders: pick which side of the equation you’re tackling first — government or commercial — and focus there. The derivative business line can come later.

Jeff Crusey · General Partner, Ice9 Capital EP. 91 · 27:35

“I’ve seen a lot of companies try to become dual-use because their investors say, oh, we need to diversify revenue, so you need to start doing all this commercial stuff too. When sometimes that doesn’t make any sense.”

Crusey’s warning: investor pressure to force dual-use is one of the most common ways early defense companies lose focus.

Zach Shore · CRO, Hermeus EP. 87 · 06:58

“Our end market, our customer is the Department of Defense and allied partner nations, and building unmanned systems that can fly high and fast in pursuit of national security.”

Hermeus is explicitly defense-first. Shore notes commercial spillover is welcome but not the strategy — the discipline of one customer kept the company moving.

Will Edwards · Founder & CEO, Firehawk EP. 86 · 28:01

“Worse than not winning a contract is winning a contract and not delivering on that contract.”

Firehawk is also defense-first. Edwards’ point: when government is your customer, the bar is delivery, not optionality. That’s incompatible with hedging across two markets at once.
